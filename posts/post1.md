# Blog Post 1: Early Ideation

When reading the design brief, I interpreted a ‘community hub’ as more than a simple social media platform. I thought of it as a digital space where people can connect through shared interests, routines and experiences. This made me think about what kinds of communities I could focus on for this design brief. 

Initially, my teammate and I explored the idea of making a location-based social app where users could connect with other people nearby to play sports or simply make friends. Rather than focusing only on athletes, we thought that casual sports hobbyists or beginners could use this app to find teammates or improve their sportsmanship and skills. However, after a quick Google search, there were many existing similar platforms. We then briefly shifted our idea towards a platform for solo travellers to find places to visit, meet new people, and exchange recommendations with other travellers. However, this raised many ethical and safety concerns, particularly in exposing user locations to strangers and encouraging interaction between unknown individuals in a foreign country.

From there, we shifted our thinking towards communities shaped by accessibility, routine, and time. We became interested in Sydney’s after-hours economy and the general experience of people whose lifestyles exist outside traditional 9–5 business hours. This idea sparked from our personal frustration with how early many businesses close in Sydney, especially when compared to cities in Asia where services and local businesses remain open until much later in the night. I also realised that some local businesses that operate late struggle to find customers if they are not already well-known. For example, there is a nail technician in my neighbourhood who continues running her business from home after finishing her 9–5 job at the nail salon. However, due to the lack of marketing and the right platform, I only knew about her business through word of mouth. This observation led us to think about a community hub for people who are only available after work hours, such as office workers, students, or individuals balancing multiple jobs. 

At this stage, we did not want the platform to function purely as a marketplace or advertising space. One thing I am still exploring is how the design could encourage genuine community interaction rather than only transactions between users and businesses. Possible ideas could involve recommendations, sharing experiences, or local discussions that help smaller businesses gain visibility through community engagement rather than only paid promotion.

Moving forward, I want to continue researching existing social platforms that may relate to our target audience, while also exploring the core functions and ethical considerations required for the platform. I am particularly interested in how the design could encourage genuine interaction rather than becoming overly transactional or commercialised.
